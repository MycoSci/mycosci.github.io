## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Family  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Leotia lubrica_ (for the type genus _Leotia_)  
* **Authority & year:** Corda, 1842  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Gelatinous or fleshy apothecia, capitate or club-shaped fruiting bodies, inoperculate asci, soft textures  

## Discovery & Naming
The family **Leotiaceae** was originally described by **August Karl Joseph Corda** in 1842. It is named after the genus _Leotia_, from Greek roots meaning "smooth" or "glossy," referring to the texture of the fruiting bodies. Historically, many fungi with small, gelatinous fruiting bodies were lumped into Leotiaceae, but molecular phylogenetics in recent decades have clarified and narrowed the family’s definition. Today, it is centered around a small number of genera sharing clear morphological and genetic features.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Leotiaceae** fungi are quite distinctive:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Soft, gelatinous, or fleshy fruiting bodies often shaped like tiny caps on stems.
  - Colors range from yellow and green to orange and brown.
  - Cap surfaces may be smooth, wrinkled, or slightly lobed.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Inoperculate, unitunicate asci (no lid opening).
  - Spores are hyaline (colorless), smooth, typically elliptical to fusiform.
  - Paraphyses are abundant, often embedded in gelatinous tissue.

**Synapomorphies** include the cap-and-stipe morphology, jelly-like texture, and inoperculate asci, distinguishing Leotiaceae from other Leotiomycetes families.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular analyses (using ITS, LSU, and protein-coding genes like RPB2) confirm **Leotiaceae** as a monophyletic group within **Leotiales**.

Key genera currently recognized in Leotiaceae include:

- _Leotia_ (e.g., _Leotia lubrica_, "jelly babies")
- _Microglossum_ (sometimes placed nearby but subject to ongoing taxonomic revision)

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Leotiales
 └── Leotiaceae
      ├── Leotia
      └── Microglossum (tentative)
```
Leotiaceae likely originated during the late Cretaceous or early Cenozoic (~70–50 million years ago), with species adapting to moist forest environments.

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Moist, shaded woodlands; mossy forest floors; humid temperate ecosystems.
- **Substrates:** Soil rich in organic material, decaying plant matter, occasionally among mosses.
- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic; playing roles in decomposition and nutrient cycling.

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan, though especially common in temperate regions of North America, Europe, and Asia.

Leotiaceae fungi are often indicators of healthy, undisturbed forest ecosystems.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
Members of **Leotiaceae**, particularly _Leotia lubrica_, are visually striking and often featured in field guides and nature photography due to their unusual gelatinous forms.

- **Edibility:** Generally considered inedible due to poor texture, though not toxic.
- **Ecological importance:** Important decomposers contributing to soil formation and nutrient turnover in forests.

Although they do not have significant economic impacts, their role in ecosystem health and their aesthetic appeal make them favorites among mycologists and mushroom enthusiasts.
