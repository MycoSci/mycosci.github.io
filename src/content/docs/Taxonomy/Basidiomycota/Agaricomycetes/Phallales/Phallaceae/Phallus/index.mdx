## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Phallus impudicus_  
* **Authority & year:** Junius ex L., 1753  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Classic stinkhorn fungi, phallic-shaped fruitbodies, slimy gleba covering the head, foul odor to attract insects  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Phallus** was formalized by **Carl Linnaeus** in 1753, though the name and recognition predate him. _Phallus_ comes directly from Greek, meaning "penis," reflecting the unmistakable shape of the mature fruitbodies.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Phallus** species are the quintessential "stinkhorns," known for their dramatic and odorous displays:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies arise from gelatinous underground "eggs" and grow rapidly into tall, erect structures.
  - Mature structures consist of a hollow, spongy, cylindrical stipe topped by a bell-shaped or rounded cap.
  - The cap is coated in olive-brown slimy gleba that emits a strong, often offensive odor to attract insects (especially flies).
  - Bright white, pink, or orange stipes contrast with darker gleba.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are club-shaped, four-spored.
  - Spores are smooth, hyaline, ellipsoid.
  - Clamp connections are absent.

**Synapomorphies** include rapid growth from a gelatinous egg, slimy odoriferous gleba at maturity, and insect-based spore dispersal.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) confirm **Phallus** as a key genus within Phallaceae, closely related to _Mutinus_, _Clathrus_, and _Lysurus_.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Phallaceae
 ├── Phallus
 ├── Clathrus
 ├── Lysurus
 └── Mutinus
```
The genus likely evolved adaptations for exploiting saprotrophic niches and rapid insect-mediated spore dispersal, especially in tropical and temperate environments.

Important species include:

- _Phallus impudicus_ (common stinkhorn)
- _Phallus hadriani_ (sand stinkhorn)
- _Phallus duplicatus_ (netted stinkhorn)

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Forest litter, gardens, grasslands, mulch beds, and urban parks.
- **Substrates:** Soil, rich humus, decaying wood, compost heaps.

- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic, breaking down organic material.

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan; found worldwide, particularly in temperate, tropical, and subtropical regions.

Species typically emerge rapidly following warm, rainy conditions.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Phallus** fungi are among the most culturally and ecologically fascinating fungi:

- **Edibility:**
  - Immature "eggs" of some species (_e.g., Phallus impudicus_) are eaten in parts of Europe and Asia before the odor develops.
  - Mature stinkhorns are generally avoided due to their strong smell.

- **Ecological importance:**
  - Play a critical role in recycling organic matter in soils.
  - Important in ecological interactions, supporting insect communities that aid in spore dispersal.

- **Cultural relevance:**
  - Historically associated with fertility, scandal, and mystery in folklore.
  - Frequently referenced in literature, art, and popular media due to their suggestive appearance.

Their iconic forms, rapid life cycles, and ecological ingenuity make _Phallus_ one of the most instantly recognizable and studied fungal genera.
