## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Order  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Phallus impudicus_ (for the type genus _Phallus_)

* **Diagnostic keywords:** Gasteroid fungi, foul-smelling slimy gleba, spongy to rubbery fruitbodies, saprotrophic  

## Discovery & Naming
The order **Phallales** was described by **Eduard Fischer** in 1898 to encompass the so-called "stinkhorn" fungi, notable for their bizarre shapes and strong odors. The name _Phallales_ derives from the Greek _phallos_, meaning "phallus," due to the resemblance of many fruitbodies to reproductive organs.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Phallales** fungi are among the most distinctive and unusual of all fungi:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies are often phallic, latticed, or star-like structures.
  - They emerge rapidly from a gelatinous "egg" stage.
  - Surfaces are coated with foul-smelling, slimy gleba when mature, attracting insects for spore dispersal.
  - Coloration is variable — white, orange, greenish, pink, or even netted in vibrant colors.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are typically four-spored.
  - Spores are smooth, hyaline, elliptical.
  - Clamp connections are absent.

**Synapomorphies** include gasteroid development (enclosed spore development), slimy spore mass (gleba), and spore dispersal via insects (mycophagy).

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) confirm **Phallales** as a distinct lineage within Agaricomycetes, separate from other gasteroid orders such as Geastrales or Boletales.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Agaricomycetes
 ├── Geastrales
 ├── Boletales
 ├── Phallales
 └── Others
```
The group likely represents an adaptation for rapid emergence and insect-mediated spore dispersal in warm, humid environments.

Major families:

- _Phallaceae_ (true stinkhorns)
- _Clathraceae_ (lattice stinkhorns)
- _Lysuraceae_ (tropical stinkhorns)

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Forests, grasslands, gardens, tropical woodlands; often in moist soils rich in organic matter.
- **Substrates:** Soil, leaf litter, compost, decaying wood.
- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic, breaking down dead plant material.

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan; especially abundant in tropical, subtropical, and temperate regions.

Species often emerge after heavy rains, sometimes growing overnight.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Phallales** fungi are famous for their strange forms and odors:

- **Edibility:**
  - Some species (_e.g., immature eggs of Phallus impudicus, Mutinus spp._) are edible before maturation and are consumed in parts of Asia and Europe.
  - Mature fruitbodies are typically avoided due to strong odors.

- **Ecological importance:**
  - Important saprotrophs in forest ecosystems, aiding nutrient cycling and soil health.
  - Their insect-mediated spore dispersal represents a remarkable ecological innovation.

- **Scientific and cultural relevance:**
  - Frequently featured in folklore, art, and popular culture due to their provocative forms.
  - Studied for rapid fungal development and insect-fungus interactions.

Their bizarre beauty, ecological efficiency, and unforgettable forms make _Phallales_ one of the most remarkable fungal orders.
