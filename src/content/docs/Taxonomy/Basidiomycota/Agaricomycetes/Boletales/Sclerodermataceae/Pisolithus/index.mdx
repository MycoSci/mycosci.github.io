## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Pisolithus arhizus_  
* **Authority & year:** Alb. & Schwein., 1805  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Gasteroid (puffball-like) fungi, tough outer skin, internal spore packets (peridioles), brown to black gleba, ectomycorrhizal  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Pisolithus** was formally described by **Johann Albert von Schweinitz** and **Johann Baptista von Albertini** in 1805. The name is derived from Greek _pisos_ ("pea") and _lithos_ ("stone"), referring to the granular appearance of the developing internal spores (peridioles).

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Pisolithus** species are distinctive and easy to recognize among earthball-like fungi:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies are globose to club-shaped, large, often roughened or cracked, brownish to black when mature.
  - Outer layers (peridium) are thick and tough, protecting the spore mass inside.
  - Gleba is initially firm and pale, later fragmenting into dark powdery masses of spore-filled peridioles.
  - No visible stipe; fruiting bodies are often partly buried in soil.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Spores are thick-walled, brown, roughly spherical to slightly elliptical, often ornamented with spines or warts.
  - Basidia are poorly differentiated; spores are produced directly in the gleba tissue.
  - Clamp connections are absent.

**Synapomorphies** include tough outer skin, internal pea-like spore packets, dark gleba, and ectomycorrhizal lifestyles.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) place **Pisolithus** firmly within Sclerodermataceae, distinct from _Scleroderma_ and _Astraeus_.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Sclerodermataceae
 ├── Scleroderma
 ├── Pisolithus
 └── Astraeus
```
The genus likely evolved in nutrient-poor and disturbed soils, where sturdy, animal-dispersed fruitbodies confer advantages.

Important species include:

- _Pisolithus arhizus_ (commonly called the dyeball)
- _Pisolithus tinctorius_

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Sandy soils, disturbed ground, mine tailings, forest plantations, arid and semi-arid areas.
- **Substrates:** Soil, particularly poor or compacted soils.
- **Trophic modes:** Ectomycorrhizal, partnering with pines, oaks, eucalypts, and other trees.

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan; abundant in Australia, North and South America, Europe, Africa, and Asia.

Species often fruit after rains but can persist in dry conditions thanks to their robust structure.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Pisolithus** fungi are highly valued for their ecological contributions:

- **Edibility:**
  - Not considered edible due to tough, fibrous textures and poor taste.

- **Ecological and economic importance:**
  - Widely used in forestry and land reclamation to inoculate tree seedlings with beneficial mycorrhizae (_e.g.,_ _Pisolithus tinctorius_).
  - Improve tree survival rates in hostile environments like deserts, mining sites, and degraded lands.

- **Scientific relevance:**
  - Models for studying fungal adaptations to extreme soils and drought resistance.
  - Important in understanding fungal-mediated soil restoration and forestry biotechnology.

Their rugged endurance, crucial mycorrhizal partnerships, and role in ecological restoration projects make _Pisolithus_ one of the most impactful genera in applied mycology.
