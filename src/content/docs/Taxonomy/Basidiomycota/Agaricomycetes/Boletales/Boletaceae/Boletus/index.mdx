# Boletus <Badge variant="note" text="Genus" />

## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Boletus edulis_  
* **Authority & year:** L., 1753  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Fleshy boletes with thick caps, brown to olive spore prints, reticulate stipes, ectomycorrhizal  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Boletus** was first formally described by **Carl Linnaeus** in 1753. The name comes from Latin _boletus_, derived from Greek _bolites_, traditionally meaning "mushroom" and particularly edible ones, referencing their prized culinary status.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Boletus** species are quintessential boletes, featuring robust forms and characteristic traits:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Caps are convex to broad and flat, smooth, velvety, or slightly sticky.
  - Pore surfaces (underside) are whitish when young, maturing to yellow, olive, or brownish, rarely bruising blue.
  - Stipes are stout, thick, often reticulate (netted) toward the apex.
  - Spore prints are olive-brown to brown.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are four-spored, club-shaped.
  - Spores are smooth, brown in deposit, elongate to subfusiform.
  - Clamp connections are absent.

**Synapomorphies** include fleshy, pore-bearing fruitbodies, reticulate stipes (often), and olive-brown spore prints.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU, rpb1, rpb2) reveal that the traditional broad concept of **Boletus** was polyphyletic. Many species once included (e.g., blue-staining boletes) are now reassigned to other genera (_Rubroboletus_, _Butyriboletus_, etc.). _Boletus_ sensu stricto now refers mainly to non-staining, pale-fleshed species like _Boletus edulis_ and its close relatives.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Boletaceae
 ├── Boletus (sensu stricto)
 ├── Rubroboletus
 ├── Butyriboletus
 ├── Aureoboletus
 └── Tylopilus
```
The genus evolved alongside ectomycorrhizal trees (especially pines, oaks, and beeches) during the Cenozoic.

Important species include:

- _Boletus edulis_ (king bolete, porcini)
- _Boletus reticulatus_ (summer bolete)
- _Boletus aereus_ (bronze bolete)

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Mixed and coniferous forests, deciduous woods, meadows near trees.
- **Substrates:** Soil, always associated with living ectomycorrhizal tree roots.
- **Trophic modes:** Obligate ectomycorrhizal.

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan; widely distributed across Europe, Asia, North America, and introduced elsewhere.

Species fruit prolifically after rains, particularly in late summer and autumn.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Boletus** fungi, particularly _Boletus edulis_, are some of the most important culinary mushrooms globally:

- **Edibility:**
  - _Boletus edulis_ is highly prized for its nutty, savory flavor and firm texture, used fresh, dried, or preserved.
  - Forms an essential ingredient in European, Chinese, and international cuisine.

- **Economic significance:**
  - Wild harvesting of porcini supports major seasonal industries in Europe, North America, and China.

- **Ecological significance:**
  - Critical symbionts with forest trees, enhancing nutrient and water uptake and contributing to forest health.

- **Cultural relevance:**
  - Celebrated in folklore, mushroom festivals, gastronomy, and traditional foraging cultures.

Their prestige as gourmet mushrooms and vital ecological partners make _Boletus_ an iconic genus in both culinary and natural history.
