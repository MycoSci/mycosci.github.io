## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Austroboletus dictyotus_  
* **Authority & year:** (Cooke & Massee) Wolfe, 1979  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Medium-sized boletes, netted (reticulate) stipes, often dry caps, brown spore prints, ectomycorrhizal  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Austroboletus** was formalized by **Carl B. Wolfe** in 1979, based on species previously grouped within _Boletus_. The name means "southern bolete," reflecting the high diversity of the genus in the Southern Hemisphere, particularly Australia and tropical Asia.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Austroboletus** species share a suite of distinct traits:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Caps are convex to plane, often dry and sometimes cracked or finely roughened.
  - Colors range from brownish, reddish, pinkish, to grayish hues.
  - Hymenophores are poroid; pore surfaces are white, cream, pinkish, or brownish.
  - Stipes are prominently reticulate (net-like surface texture) and typically dry.
  - Spore prints are brown.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are club-shaped, typically four-spored.
  - Spores are ornamented (warty, ridged, or pitted) and brown in deposit.
  - Clamp connections are absent.

**Synapomorphies** include ornamented spores, reticulate stipes, brown spore prints, and dry fruiting body textures.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU, RPB2) place **Austroboletus** within Boletaceae but distinct from core _Boletus_ and related genera like _Tylopilus_ and _Rubroboletus_.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Boletaceae
 ├── Boletus
 ├── Tylopilus
 ├── Rubroboletus
 ├── Aureoboletus
 └── Austroboletus
```
The genus likely diversified in the Gondwanan landmasses, spreading across tropical and subtropical ecosystems with ectomycorrhizal tree partners.

Important species include:

- _Austroboletus dictyotus_ (type species)
- _Austroboletus gracilis_
- _Austroboletus subflavidus_

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Forests, woodlands, often with ectomycorrhizal trees like oaks, beeches, and tropical dipterocarps.
- **Substrates:** Soil in association with living tree roots.
- **Trophic modes:** Ectomycorrhizal.

- **Geographic range:** Primarily tropical and subtropical regions in South America, Australia, Southeast Asia, and parts of Africa.

Species often fruit during rainy seasons in warm, humid climates.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Austroboletus** fungi are important in ecological studies but have modest culinary fame:

- **Edibility:**
  - Some species (e.g., _Austroboletus subflavidus_) are edible but not widely consumed due to their tough texture or bitter taste.
  - Accurate identification is important, as some related boletes can cause gastric upset.

- **Ecological significance:**
  - Key players in tropical and subtropical forest nutrient cycles via ectomycorrhizal symbiosis.

- **Scientific relevance:**
  - Studied for their unique spore ornamentation and evolutionary relationships within Boletaceae.

Their rugged forms, distinctive netted stems, and crucial forest roles make _Austroboletus_ a fascinating genus for both field mycologists and forest ecologists.
