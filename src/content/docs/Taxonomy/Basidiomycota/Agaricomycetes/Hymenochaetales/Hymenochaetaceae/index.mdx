## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Family  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Hymenochaete rubiginosa_ (for the type genus _Hymenochaete_)  
* **Authority & year:** Lév., 1846  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Tough wood-decaying fungi, crust-like to bracket-shaped, often with setae, brown rot or white rot agents, mostly saprotrophic, some parasitic  

## Discovery & Naming
The family **Hymenochaetaceae** was established by **Joseph-Henri Léveillé** in 1846. It is named after the type genus _Hymenochaete_, which means "membrane bristle" in Greek, reflecting the often bristly, setae-bearing surfaces of its members.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Hymenochaetaceae** fungi are tough and persistent wood colonizers:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies range from thin crusts (resupinate) to thick, bracket-shaped polypores.
  - Surface colors are typically brown, reddish-brown, black, or yellowish.
  - Hymenophores can be smooth, cracked, finely hairy, or poroid.
  - Many species bear setae — thick-walled, bristle-like sterile structures visible on the hymenium.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are club-shaped, typically four-spored.
  - Spores are smooth, hyaline to pale brown, ellipsoid.
  - Clamp connections are usually absent.

**Synapomorphies** include the presence of thick-walled setae, tough textures, brownish hues, and lignin-degrading abilities.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) support **Hymenochaetaceae** as a core family within Hymenochaetales, distinct from typical polypores and corticioid fungi.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Hymenochaetales
 └── Hymenochaetaceae
      ├── Hymenochaete
      ├── Phellinus
      └── Inonotus
```
The family likely represents one of the oldest lineages of efficient white rot fungi, capable of decomposing both lignin and cellulose.

Major genera:

- _Hymenochaete_ (bristly crust fungi)
- _Phellinus_ (large, woody shelf fungi)
- _Inonotus_ (soft-fleshed polypores)

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Standing dead trees, fallen logs, live trees (as parasites), stumps, and coarse woody debris.
- **Substrates:** Hardwood and softwood species.
- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic (decomposing dead wood) or parasitic (causing heart rot in living trees).

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan; especially diverse in temperate and tropical forests worldwide.

Species can fruit throughout the year and persist for multiple seasons or even decades.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Hymenochaetaceae** fungi have strong ecological and forestry significance:

- **Edibility:**
  - Not edible — fruitbodies are woody, leathery, and lack culinary value.

- **Ecological importance:**
  - Essential for nutrient cycling by breaking down lignified plant tissues.
  - Some species (_e.g., Phellinus_ spp.) are important forest pathogens causing heart rot and structural weakening of trees.

- **Scientific and economic relevance:**
  - Studied extensively for lignin-degrading enzymes and white rot decay mechanisms.
  - Some species (_e.g., Inonotus obliquus_, known as "chaga") are of medicinal interest and widely harvested for their antioxidant and purported therapeutic properties.

Their toughness, deep ecological roles, and fascinating enzyme systems make _Hymenochaetaceae_ a cornerstone family in fungal ecology and forest pathology.
