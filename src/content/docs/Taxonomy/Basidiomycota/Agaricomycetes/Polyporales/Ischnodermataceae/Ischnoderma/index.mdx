## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Ischnoderma resinosum_  
* **Authority & year:** P. Karst., 1879  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Tough, dark-colored bracket fungi, poroid undersurface, resinous exudates when fresh, white rot decomposers  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Ischnoderma** was described by **Petter Adolf Karsten** in 1879. The name combines Greek _ischnos_ ("dry" or "thin") and _derma_ ("skin"), referring to the often leathery to resinous, thin outer layer of young fruitbodies.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Ischnoderma** species are robust fungi, often striking in color and texture:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies are large, bracket-shaped (shelf-like) or lobed.
  - Upper surfaces are velvety when young, becoming smooth and often cracked with age; colored dark brown, chocolate, or blackish.
  - Fresh fruitbodies sometimes exude a brownish, resinous liquid when cut.
  - Pore surfaces are white to buff, bruising brownish with age.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are club-shaped, four-spored.
  - Spores are smooth, hyaline, ellipsoid to cylindrical.
  - Clamp connections are usually absent.

**Synapomorphies** include tough bracket forms, dark mature colors, resinous exudation, and white rot decay.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) place **Ischnoderma** as the core genus of the family Ischnodermataceae within the Polyporales, distinct from Polyporaceae and Fomitopsidaceae.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Polyporales
 ├── Polyporaceae
 ├── Fomitopsidaceae
 └── Ischnodermataceae
      └── Ischnoderma
```
The genus likely evolved specialized strategies for rapidly colonizing and breaking down hardwood logs and stumps.

Important species include:

- _Ischnoderma resinosum_ (resinous polypore)
- _Ischnoderma benzoinum_

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Dead hardwood trunks and logs; rarely found on softwoods.
- **Substrates:** Decaying wood, especially of hardwoods like oak, maple, and birch.
- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic, causing white rot by degrading lignin and cellulose.

- **Geographic range:** Widely distributed in temperate forests across North America, Europe, and Asia.

Species typically fruit in autumn and persist through winter, sometimes visible under snow.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Ischnoderma** fungi are important in forest nutrient cycles:

- **Edibility:**
  - Young fruitbodies of _Ischnoderma resinosum_ are sometimes considered edible when tender, though they toughen quickly and are not widely consumed.

- **Ecological importance:**
  - Efficient decomposers of hardwood material.
  - Contribute to nutrient cycling and soil health by accelerating deadwood breakdown.

- **Scientific relevance:**
  - Studied for their enzymatic capabilities in white rot decomposition, particularly for biotechnological applications in lignin degradation and biopulping.

Their dark beauty, resilience across seasons, and crucial ecological roles make _Ischnoderma_ a fascinating genus among wood-decaying fungi.
