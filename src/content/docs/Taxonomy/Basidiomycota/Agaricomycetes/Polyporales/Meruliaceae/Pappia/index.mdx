* **Diagnostic keywords:** Resupinate (crust-like) fungi, smooth to wrinkled hymenophores, soft to leathery texture, white rot decomposers  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Pappia** was described by **Otso Miettinen** and **Mario Rajchenberg** in 2012, following molecular and morphological studies that separated these species from other Meruliaceae members. The name honors Finnish mycologist **Pekka Pappinen** for his contributions to polypore research.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Pappia** fungi are understated but important members of forest ecosystems:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies are resupinate (crust-like), thin, and closely attached to the substrate.
  - Hymenophore surfaces are smooth to finely wrinkled or cracked, often pale cream, buff, or light brown.
  - Texture is soft to leathery when fresh, drying brittle.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are club-shaped, typically four-spored.
  - Spores are smooth, hyaline, ellipsoid to cylindrical.
  - Clamp connections are present.

**Synapomorphies** include resupinate growth, soft textures, smooth to wrinkled hymenophores, and white rot decay.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic analyses (ITS, LSU) support **Pappia** as a distinct genus within the Meruliaceae family of Polyporales, closely allied with other crust-like white rot fungi such as _Phlebia_.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Meruliaceae
 ├── Phlebia
 ├── Climacodon
 └── Pappia
```
The genus likely evolved to specialize in fast colonization of fallen logs and forest litter, aiding early-stage wood decomposition.

Important species:

- _Pappia radicata_ (type species)

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Decaying logs, fallen branches, and stumps, mainly in moist forest environments.
- **Substrates:** Dead hardwood and occasionally conifer wood.
- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic, causing white rot by breaking down lignin and cellulose.

- **Geographic range:** Known from temperate forests of South America, but likely under-recorded and more widespread.

Species are typically overlooked due to their small, crust-like appearances but are essential decomposers.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Pappia** fungi are scientifically and ecologically important:

- **Edibility:**
  - Not edible — fruitbodies are too thin and tough for culinary use.

- **Ecological importance:**
  - Major agents of nutrient cycling, breaking down coarse woody debris into humus.
  - Critical for maintaining soil fertility and supporting complex forest food webs.

- **Scientific relevance:**
  - Studied for understanding early fungal colonization of deadwood and enzymatic profiles associated with efficient white rot decay.

Their subtle presence, crucial ecological role, and contribution to forest resilience make _Pappia_ a genus worth recognizing among crust fungi.
