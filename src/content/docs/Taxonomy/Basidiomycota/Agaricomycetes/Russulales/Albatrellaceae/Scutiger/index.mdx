## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Scutiger pes-caprae_  
* **Authority & year:** Fr., 1838  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Fleshy polypores, centrally-stalked or sessile, soft to tough texture, large pores, white rot decomposers  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Scutiger** was established by **Elias Magnus Fries** in 1838. The name comes from Latin _scutiger_ ("shield-bearer"), reflecting the shield-like cap structures of the fruitbodies.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Scutiger** species are robust and often resemble soft polypores:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies are centrally or eccentrically stalked, with broad, shield-like caps.
  - Cap surfaces are smooth to scaly, colored white, gray, brown, or pinkish.
  - Hymenophores are poroid with relatively large, angular pores.
  - Flesh is thick, soft when fresh, firming with age.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are club-shaped, typically four-spored.
  - Spores are smooth, hyaline, broadly ellipsoid.
  - Clamp connections are often present.

**Synapomorphies** include fleshy polypore forms, large pore structures, and white rot capabilities.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) place **Scutiger** within Albatrellaceae of the Russulales, closely related to _Albatrellus_ and _Jahnoporus_, although some species have shifted taxonomically over time.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Albatrellaceae
 ├── Albatrellus
 ├── Jahnoporus
 └── Scutiger
```
The genus represents a lineage adapted to forest floor decomposition, specializing in moist wood and soil substrates.

Important species include:

- _Scutiger pes-caprae_ (goat’s foot polypore)
- _Scutiger hispidellus_ (a rarer species)

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Coniferous and mixed forests; commonly associated with mossy or humus-rich soils, near decaying wood.
- **Substrates:** Soil enriched by decayed wood or directly on roots and buried wood.
- **Trophic modes:** Primarily saprotrophic, occasionally weakly ectomycorrhizal.

- **Geographic range:** Found mainly in temperate forests of North America, Europe, and parts of Asia.

Species often fruit in late summer and autumn, forming solitary or small clusters.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Scutiger** fungi are important for ecosystem functions but have little culinary use:

- **Edibility:**
  - Not widely considered edible; textures are often too fibrous and pores too coarse for culinary appeal.

- **Ecological importance:**
  - Important early decomposers of fallen wood and rich forest soils.
  - Help enrich soil and open space for later successional fungi and plants.

- **Scientific relevance:**
  - Studied for their wood-decay abilities and adaptation to high-moisture forest environments.

Their rugged shield-like caps, soft yet durable textures, and quiet ecological work make _Scutiger_ an important, if often overlooked, genus in temperate forests.
