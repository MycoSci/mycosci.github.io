## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Calocybe gambosa_  
* **Authority & year:** Kühner ex Donk, 1962  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Medium-sized fleshy mushrooms, thick white to cream gills, dry caps, white spore prints, mostly spring-fruiting  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Calocybe** was formally described by **Marinus Anton Donk** in 1962, refining the earlier concept by **Robert Kühner**. The name combines the Greek _kalos_ ("beautiful") and _cybe_ ("head"), referring to the attractive, fleshy fruitbodies. _Calocybe_ includes several edible and ecologically important species, especially in grasslands and woodland edges.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Calocybe** species share distinctive features compared to related groups:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Caps are convex to flat, dry to slightly greasy, smooth or finely fibrous.
  - Colors are typically pale: white, cream, buff, or slightly yellowish.
  - Gills are thick, crowded, and white to cream-colored.
  - Stipes are stout, solid, and usually smooth.
  - Spore prints are white.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are club-shaped, producing four spores.
  - Spores are smooth, hyaline, ellipsoid to slightly oblong.
  - Clamp connections are usually present.

**Synapomorphies** include fleshy basidiocarps with dry, pale caps, thick crowded gills, and white spores.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) place **Calocybe** within Lyophyllaceae, related to _Lyophyllum_, _Tephrocybe_, and _Myochromella_.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Lyophyllaceae
 ├── Lyophyllum
 ├── Calocybe
 ├── Tephrocybe
 └── Myochromella
```
_Calocybe_ likely diversified in temperate grassland and woodland margins during the Cenozoic.

Important species include:

- _Calocybe gambosa_ (St. George’s mushroom)
- _Calocybe carnea_ (pink domecap)
- _Calocybe ionides_

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Grasslands, meadows, pastures, forest edges, and old gardens.
- **Substrates:** Soil, particularly nutrient-rich or humus-rich environments.
- **Trophic modes:** Mostly saprotrophic, though some species may form weak symbiotic associations with grasses.

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan; species occur widely across Europe, North America, Asia, and Australasia.

Species like _Calocybe gambosa_ fruit reliably in early spring, often associated with traditional seasonal mushroom hunts.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Calocybe** fungi are culturally and gastronomically significant:

- **Edibility:**
  - _Calocybe gambosa_ is a prized edible in Europe (especially in Britain and France), known for its rich flavor and early fruiting season.
  - Other species are edible but less well known or are consumed locally.

- **Culinary significance:**
  - _Calocybe gambosa_ is traditionally harvested around St. George’s Day (April 23rd) in Europe, earning it the common name "St. George’s mushroom."
  - Valued for its dense, meaty texture and sweet, nutty taste.

- **Ecological importance:**
  - Contributes to organic matter decomposition and soil nutrient cycling.

- **Conservation relevance:**
  - In some areas, suitable grassland habitats for _Calocybe_ species are threatened by agricultural intensification.

Their dependable spring fruiting, pleasant flavor, and long association with European culinary traditions make _Calocybe_ a beloved genus among mushroom hunters.
