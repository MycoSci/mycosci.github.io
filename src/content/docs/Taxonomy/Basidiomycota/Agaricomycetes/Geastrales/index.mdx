**Taxonomic hierarchy:** **Phylum:** Basidiomycota › **Class:** Agaricomycetes › **Order:** Geastrales

## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Order  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Geastrum triplex_ (for the type genus _Geastrum_)  
* **Authority & year:** Gäumann, 1926  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Gasteroid fungi (earthstars), star-like opening of fruitbodies, brown spore masses, saprotrophic  

## Discovery & Naming
The order **Geastrales** was formally described by **Ernst Gäumann** in 1926 to encompass the distinctive star-shaped gasteroid fungi known as earthstars. The name comes from Greek _gea_ ("earth") and _astron_ ("star"), perfectly capturing the striking morphology of these fungi.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
**Geastrales** fungi are known for their unique and dramatic fruitbody structure:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Fruiting bodies start as roughly spherical structures (peridioles) that split open into star-like rays upon maturity and exposure to humidity.
  - Central spore sac (endoperidium) remains intact, containing brownish powdery spores.
  - Outer layers (exoperidium) often crack into multiple rays that arch backward.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Basidia are typically club-shaped, producing spores internally before release.
  - Spores are ornamented (warted or spiny), thick-walled, brown in deposit.
  - Clamp connections are absent.

**Synapomorphies** include star-like opening mechanisms, dark ornamented spores, and gasteroid reproduction.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (ITS, LSU) support **Geastrales** as a monophyletic lineage within the Agaricomycetes, distinct from other gasteroid orders such as Phallales (stinkhorns) and Boletales (false earthstars like _Astraeus_).

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Agaricomycetes
 ├── Agaricales
 ├── Boletales
 ├── Geastrales
 └── Others
```
The order likely evolved adaptations for wind- or rain-assisted spore dispersal in dry or fluctuating climates.

Major families:

- _Geastraceae_ (the primary and defining family of true earthstars)

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Forest floors, sandy soils, grasslands, dry woodlands.
- **Substrates:** Soil, rich humus, decomposing plant debris.
- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic, decomposing organic matter.

- **Geographic range:** Cosmopolitan; found across temperate, tropical, and semi-arid regions worldwide.

Species fruit during wet seasons, with rays opening in response to humidity changes.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Geastrales** fungi are ecologically important and culturally iconic:

- **Edibility:**
  - Generally considered **inedible** due to tough texture and lack of culinary value.

- **Ecological significance:**
  - Contribute to organic matter decomposition and soil formation processes.
  - Help indicate healthy ecosystems, especially sandy or well-drained soils.

- **Cultural relevance:**
  - Featured in folklore, traditional stories, and even nature symbolism because of their "earthstar" appearance.
  - Admired for their dramatic opening and closing movements in response to moisture.

Their striking "stars of the forest floor," humble ecological service, and ancient evolutionary heritage make _Geastrales_ one of the most fascinating fungal orders in the natural world.
