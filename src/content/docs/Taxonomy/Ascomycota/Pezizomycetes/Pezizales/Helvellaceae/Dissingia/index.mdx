## Quick ID
* **Rank:** Genus  
* **Type species (if applicable):** _Dissingia leucomelaena_  
* **Authority & year:** N.S. Weber, 1972  
* **Diagnostic keywords:** Saddle-shaped ascocarps, ribbed and hollow stipes, fleshy to brittle textures, operculate asci  

## Discovery & Naming
The genus **Dissingia** was described by **Nancy Smith Weber** in 1972. It honors the Danish mycologist **Henry Dissing**, recognized for his extensive work on Pezizales. _Dissingia_ was erected to accommodate species previously placed in _Helvella_ but shown to differ morphologically and genetically, especially based on ribbing patterns and spore details. Molecular studies later supported the separation of _Dissingia_ from _Helvella_, although the two remain closely related within Helvellaceae.

## Morphology & Diagnostics
Species of **Dissingia** share distinct and recognizable traits:

- **Macroscopic traits:**
  - Ascocarps are saddle-shaped or deeply lobed, sometimes resembling _Helvella_ species.
  - Stipes are prominently ribbed, hollow, and often grooved.
  - Coloration tends toward gray, white, or dark gray-brown tones.
  - Texture fleshy when fresh, becoming brittle upon drying.

- **Microscopic traits:**
  - Asci are operculate, unitunicate, and cylindrical, typically producing 8 ascospores.
  - Ascospores are smooth, hyaline (colorless), and elliptical.
  - Paraphyses are abundant, often with slightly swollen tips.

**Synapomorphies** for _Dissingia_ include the prominently ribbed stipes and the specific spore morphology, differentiating it from other Helvellaceae genera like _Helvella_.

## Phylogeny & Evolution
Molecular phylogenetic studies (based on ITS, LSU, and RPB2 sequences) firmly place **Dissingia** within **Helvellaceae** as a distinct clade separate from _Helvella_ but sharing a close evolutionary relationship.

**Simple text-based cladogram:**
```
Helvellaceae
 ├── Helvella
 ├── Balsamia
 ├── Dissingia
 └── Underwoodia
```
The divergence of _Dissingia_ likely occurred in the Mesozoic, associated with the diversification of temperate forest ecosystems where many species are now found.

Recognized species include:

- _Dissingia leucomelaena_ (formerly _Helvella leucomelaena_)
- _Dissingia confusa_

## Ecology & Distribution
- **Habitat preferences:** Moist, mossy forest soils; sometimes associated with coniferous woodlands and forest edges.
- **Substrates:** Soil rich in organic matter, sometimes near decaying logs or leaf litter.
- **Trophic modes:** Saprotrophic; decomposing organic material and contributing to soil health.

- **Geographic range:** Widespread across North America and Europe, with a preference for temperate climates; also recorded in parts of Asia.

Species typically fruit in spring and early summer, often shortly after snowmelt or seasonal rains.

## Cultural & Economic Notes
**Dissingia** fungi are not commonly collected for culinary purposes and are generally considered of low edibility value:

- **Edibility:** While not known to be highly toxic, _Dissingia_ species are usually deemed inedible due to their tough texture and low culinary appeal.
- **Ecological importance:** 
  - Important decomposers in forest ecosystems.
  - Their early fruiting helps recycle nutrients during critical periods of plant growth.

In mycology, they are valued for their morphological beauty and evolutionary importance within the Pezizales.
